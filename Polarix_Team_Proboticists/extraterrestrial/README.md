#### Why regolith mapping?

Regolith terrain mapping is a way of describing the landscape that captures the landforms, materials of which it is made, and any secondary induration. It is a useful tool for soil mapping, environmental geology, geomorphology mapping, regolith studies, planetary exploration, land systems mapping, soil ecology, groundwater studies, and mineral exploration.

#### How regolith holds the record of habitability

On Earth, clay minerals (phyllosilicates) tell us where water once was; whether in soils, rivers, or hydrothermal vents
Earth destroys its oldest crust through **plate tectonics**, so we lost evidence of the first billion years.
However, Mars, by contrast, *kept* half its crust from that era (the Noachian–Hesperian, 4.1–3.1 Gyr). These rocks record water–rock interactions frozen in time.

The variables that control which minerals are formed when rock meets water are given below

1. **Temperature & pressure** = determine reaction kinetics.
2. **Water-to-rock ratio (W/R)** = low = little water → subsurface; high = lots of water → surface weathering.
3. **Open vs. closed chemical system** — open means fluids exchange with the atmosphere; closed means isolated pores deep underground.
4. **Closed + low W/R = subsurface hydrothermal alteration.**
    
    → Fe–Mg clays, chlorite, serpentine, prehnite.
    
5. **Open + high W/R = surface weathering.**
    
    → Al-clays, oxides, and salts (kaolinite, jarosite, sulfates)

## Abstract

The extraterrestrial subsystem is Polarix's science package, designed to analyse soil and environmental samples for markers consistent with past or present life. This study presents a rover-based framework for investigating planetary regolith as a record of past habitability, with a specific focus on Mars analog environments. Regolith, comprising unconsolidated material between bedrock and atmosphere preserves geochemical and mineralogical signatures of past water–rock interactions, particularly on Mars where ancient terrains remain largely intact. This is largely due to the difference in the movement of tectonic plates of earth and of mars.

The proposed system integrates regolith-terrain mapping, in-situ physicochemical sensing, atmospheric monitoring, and molecular characterization via Raman spectroscopy to identify and assess habitability indicators. Emphasis is placed on non-destructive analytical techniques, enabling preservation of samples for future analysis while minimizing contamination risks. By combining multi-parameter environmental data (pH, electrical conductivity, moisture, nutrients) with gas sensing and mineralogical profiling, the framework enables robust interpretation of biosignature potential and environmental history.

The work demonstrates a scalable, contamination-aware methodology for autonomous planetary exploration, providing a comprehensive approach for detecting conditions conducive to microbial life.

## Introduction 

Regolith, the unconsolidated layer between bedrock and atmosphere on planetary surfaces—serves as a critical archive of environmental and geochemical history, preserving evidence of past water–rock interactions and potential biosignatures. While Earth’s early habitability record has been extensively altered by tectonic recycling and erosion, Mars retains vast exposures of ancient Noachian-Hesperian terrains, offering a uniquely well-preserved window into early planetary conditions. In this context, regolith becomes a key target for astrobiological investigation. This study presents a rover-based framework for systematically identifying and characterizing habitability indicators within Martian regolith through integrated site mapping, in-situ physicochemical analysis, atmospheric monitoring, and non-destructive molecular characterization. By prioritizing contamination-aware sampling and spectroscopic techniques such as Raman analysis, the approach enables robust detection of mineralogical and chemical signatures associated with past or present life, advancing the methodology for autonomous planetary exploration and biosignature assessment.

## Motivation

The search for extraterrestrial life requires methodologies that are both scientifically robust and operationally viable under extreme planetary conditions. Mars represents one of the most compelling targets for such investigations due to its preserved ancient crust and evidence of past aqueous environments. However, the primary challenge lies not in detecting life directly, but in identifying reliable indicators of past or present habitability within a complex and poorly constrained geochemical context.

Regolith is a scientifically strategic target because it acts as an integrative environmental archive. Unlike isolated rock samples, regolith accumulates and preserves signals from multiple processes over time, including:

1. Water–rock interactions (e.g., formation of clay minerals and evaporites)

2. Atmospheric exchange processes

3. Chemical weathering and alteration

4. Potential trapping and preservation of organic compounds

On Mars, where large regions of ancient terrain remain relatively undisturbed, regolith provides a continuous record of environmental evolution, making it ideal for reconstructing habitability conditions. Additionally, subsurface regolith offers protection from radiation and oxidative surface chemistry, increasing the likelihood of preserving fragile biosignatures.

## Chemical Testing

This project does not incorporate any type of wet orchemical testing for a wide variety of reasons:
A central limitation in astrobiology is the uncertainty of extraterrestrial biochemistry. Targeting a specific biomolecule—such as a protein—implicitly assumes that alien life shares Earth-like biochemical pathways, which is a strong and potentially invalid assumption. Even on Earth, proteins degrade rapidly under harsh environmental conditions, especially in oxidizing, radiation-rich environments like the Martian surface.

Instead, this project adopts a systems-level, multi-parameter approach, analyzing variations in:

1. Physicochemical properties (pH, salinity, moisture, nutrients)

2. Mineralogical composition (clays, sulfates, carbonates)

3. Atmospheric and trace gas dynamics (e.g., CH₄, CO, NH₃)

These parameters collectively define the habitability envelope-the range of environmental conditions that could support life. By examining spatial and temporal variations across these factors, the approach allows for:

1. Identification of environments that were once conducive to life

2. Detection of geochemical anomalies potentially linked to biological activity

3. Differentiation between abiotic and biotic processes through contextual data.

Direct biochemical assays (e.g., protein detection tests) are not optimal for planetary exploration due to several constraints:

1. Biochemical Assumptions

2. Earth-centric: assumes life uses proteins, amino acids, or similar molecules

3. May fail to detect fundamentally different life chemistries

4. Degradation and Preservation Issues

5. Organic molecules degrade under UV radiation, oxidation, and temperature extremes

6. Surface conditions on Mars are particularly hostile to biomolecule preservation

7. Contamination Risks

8. Wet chemical assays require reagents, increasing the risk of forward contamination

9. Difficult to distinguish indigenous signals from introduced contaminants

10. Limited Interpretability

11. A positive or negative result in isolation provides little context

12. Cannot distinguish between biological origin and abiotic organic synthesis.

## Methodology
The adopted methodology prioritizes non-destructive, in-situ analysis techniques, such as Raman spectroscopy and gas sensing, combined with environmental measurements. This approach offers several advantages:

1. Preservation of samples for future analysis or return missions

2. Reduced contamination risk due to minimal sample handling

3. High interpretability, as results are contextualized within environmental and geological data

4. Scalability and autonomy, enabling deployment on rover platforms

By integrating multiple independent datasets, the system enables cross-validation of findings, increasing confidence in identifying true habitability signals.
The methodology is designed to evaluate habitability through a context-based, multi-parameter investigation of regolith rather than a single biomolecule test. The workflow begins with site selection and documentation, where candidate locations are identified using regolith-terrain mapping. Each site is classified according to landform type, dominant regolith material, grain characteristics, and degree of surface induration or crusting. This provides the geological context needed to interpret whether a location has experienced deposition, weathering, aqueous alteration, or subsurface preservation processes.
After site identification, the rover performs contamination-aware subsurface sampling using an auger-based drilling mechanism. The auger rotates at a controlled speed chosen to retain material from the target depth without excessive loss during extraction. The collected sample is transferred into a self-closing iris container, and separate trays are used to keep samples from different locations isolated. This ensures sample integrity and reduces cross-contamination during sequential analysis.
The collected regolith is then subjected to in-situ physicochemical analysis. A multi-parameter soil sensor measures pH, electrical conductivity, moisture content, temperature, and macronutrients such as nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium. Dedicated pH and EC sensors operate alongside the main probe so that measurements can be cross-validated through sensor fusion. These parameters are used to assess salinity, water availability, nutrient conditions, and general geochemical suitability for habitability. Instead of relying on a single biochemical reaction, this step captures the broader environmental envelope within which life could exist or once have existed.
To further characterize the sample, the rover performs molecular and mineralogical analysis using Raman spectroscopy. Raman spectroscopy is selected because it is non-destructive and does not require chemical reagents. The technique identifies mineral phases and carbon-bearing compounds through their molecular fingerprints, allowing detection of clay minerals, evaporites, and possible organic signatures associated with past water–rock interaction and biosignature preservation. Because the method preserves the sample, the same material remains available for repeated analysis or future caching.
In parallel, the rover conducts atmospheric and gas analysis at each sampling site. Sensors record temperature, humidity, pressure, and concentrations of gases such as CO₂, CH₄, CO, NH₃, and H₂. CO₂ is used to establish baseline atmospheric context, while trace gases are monitored for anomalies that may indicate ongoing geochemical reactions or processes relevant to biology. These atmospheric measurements are correlated with the regolith data to build an integrated view of surface–atmosphere interactions.
The final stage of the methodology is integrated interpretation. Data from terrain mapping, physicochemical sensing, Raman spectra, and atmospheric analysis are combined to evaluate the habitability potential of each site. Rather than asking only whether a specific biomolecule exists, the methodology examines how mineralogy, chemistry, moisture, gas composition, and geological context vary together. This makes the approach more robust for astrobiology, since extraterrestrial life may not share Earth-like peptide-based biochemistry and could therefore escape detection by traditional protein assays such as the Biuret test. By focusing on environmental conditions, preservation potential, and non-specific chemical signatures, the methodology provides a broader and scientifically stronger framework for identifying habitability-relevant indicators in Martian regolith.
